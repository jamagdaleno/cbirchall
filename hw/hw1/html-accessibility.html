HTML for accessibility

There are several easy ways to add usability and accessibility to your webpages using just HTML. Read below for several great tips.

Step 1. Use the right HTML element

This may seem obvious, but it can be all too easy to apply a general tag when there are several semantic tags that can do the job better. For example, one could use a tags for a button, but using the semantic tag button is much more descriptive. But why does it matter?

One word: Screen readers! A descriptive tag is much more useful for those to determine what piece of content they are dealing with. Other reasons?

  • Descriptive for screen readers
  • Easier to understand
  • Better for SEO.

Step 2. Your language should be clear

For screenreaders, it may be difficult to understand the content if the website's content is not laid out in a clear, precise manner. Some general tips include: no abbreviations if possible (write out "February" instead of "Feb"), avoiding dashes ("10 to 12" instead of "10-12"), and writing acronyms out. By using these tips, you'll be able to provide easy-to-read content for everyone accessing your website.

Step 3. Apply a modern website structure

Back in the 'old days' days of web development, many would apply table layouts for their design. While it may have made sense back then, nowadays such a layout will bloat your code, your design will be inflexible, and most importantly, will create a real headache for screenreaders to understand your content.

Instead, you're much better off applying modern HTML5 semantic tags to designate your content throughout (think headers, footers, nav, sections).
